\begin{abstract}
Flat-space self-supervised learning methods---VICReg, Barlow Twins, and
SimDINO among them---prevent representation collapse with a \emph{split
recipe}: a scale-control term that keeps per-dimension variance near one
and a decorrelation term that pushes off-diagonal covariance toward zero.
The recipe is effective but ad hoc: each method instantiates the two
ingredients differently, and their relative weights are tuned empirically.
We show that these recipes are, up to small corrections, Taylor
approximations of a single basis-free object: the Kullback--Leibler
divergence between the empirical batch-feature covariance and the identity,
$\mathcal{L}_\text{covKL} = \tfrac{1}{2}(\tr(\mat{C}) - \logdet(\mat{C}) - d)$.
This object has a unique minimum at $\mat{C} = \mat{I}$---a property that
coding-rate expansions used by SimDINO and MCR\textsuperscript{2} lack---and
couples scale and structure in the natural way, obviating the need for a
separate scale anchor. Paired with a standard invariance loss and a mean
penalty, covariance-KL trains a ResNet-18 on CIFAR-10 to within striking
distance of VICReg's $k$-nearest-neighbour accuracy at matched compute
budget, and matches or exceeds SimDINO. We derive the approximation
relationship, characterize the objective's behaviour in the collapse limit,
and provide practical considerations (shrinkage, per-view application,
balance with the invariance term).
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} self-supervised learning, Gaussian matching, rate-distortion, flat-space SSL, representation geometry
